Networking & referrals

For an international candidate, one warm referral that gets her resume read beats fifty cold applications. Track every recruiter, engineer, alum, and mentor here — and attach proof of each real connection (a LinkedIn message, an email, or a referral screenshot).

How to network — the short version
  • Start warm: find UTD alumni at your target companies on LinkedIn (filter by school + company) and send a short, specific note.
  • Ask small: request a 15-minute chat about their team and what they work on — not a referral up front.
  • Give first: engage with their posts, share your own projects, be genuinely curious — be memorable for the right reasons.
  • Then ask: after a good conversation, send your resume + GitHub and ask if they'd be comfortable referring you.
  • Go where engineers are: career fairs, hackathons, ACM / AI club, and company Slack / Discord communities.
  • Always follow up within 24 hours, and log it here with proof so nothing slips.

Visa & sponsorship strategy

Han Han's status path and how to maximize the odds of long-term success. This is a plan, not legal advice — UTD's international office (ISSO) and an immigration attorney are the authorities.

Her edge: the green-card math

Because she was born in Vietnam, her employment-based green card has essentially no backlog — unlike classmates from India or China who wait 10+ years. If a FAANG employer sponsors her green card (most do), she can reach permanent residency in a few years and stop depending on the H-1B lottery entirely. This makes her easier to sponsor — lead with it when employers raise visa questions.

O-1 evidence builder (lottery-proof backup)

An O-1 "extraordinary ability" visa skips the H-1B lottery. She's already generating the raw material — make it visible and documented. Each item checked strengthens an eventual O-1 case (and her resume).

Apply smart + key links

Target companies with strong sponsorship track records, mind the deadlines, and keep status clean (no gaps — work with ISSO).
